# Chapter 1: The Journal

A lone man sat at his metal desk basked under the dim glow of a desk lamp. The window outside was pitch black though he thought he could see at least a few notable stars. Perhaps that red dot at the far edge was Mars. The sun was on the opposite side of the spacecraft which he accepted as an unbidden celestial gift. Thoughts seemed to come easier in darkness. He picked up a yellow legal pad and started to write. Words formed. 

*Simple words are enough to describe this feeling. Is it emptiness? Excitement? Melancholy? Melancholy and emptiness are similar in nature. **A particular type of ennui. **Cousins almost or rather a parent-child relationship. One begets another. A subclass maybe if you’re into object-oriented programming.*

*The window outside showed only darkness. A void deeper than what most of humanity has seen. But is it deeper than the void I felt within? I felt that there was nothing more for me to do back on Earth. Parents deceased. Relatives alive are those I barely know more than the barista at the local Starbucks. Divorced. Not talking to ex-wife except for the kids. Kids **grown** up now. Never did **connect** well with them either. May be generational gap. Maybe he’s just a **maverick** too obsessed with his own inner thoughts and hobbies and interests and travels to really connect with anyone on their level.*

*What do I know? There’s a great deal of communication that passes as “normal” social interaction which bores me. I was always the odd one out even if I was the most popular in some ways – a gala dinner in my honor for instance. I said my inspiring words and then spent the next few hours in insipid vapid conversations. Everyone saw just images of me that they thought they knew. When I tried to speak my true thoughts to family, they just thought of me as an alien. *

*Might as we**ll be **one** right? In my entire life, I have yet to meet someone who I felt truly **understands me**. Maybe that’s why I signed up for this voyage. Volunteered. Applied. To see possible real aliens or at least the remnants of their technology.*

*Humans are draining social creatures. The solution I found was to spend **less and less** time with all of them. The government must be surprised that it didn’t need to try very hard to convince me to go on this one-way trip. I knew even before they told me. There’s not enough fuel to get there and back. *

*How many others on this ship **knows** this? We have a crew of a dozen people. At least some of them must be smart enough to know this. Maybe they just don’t care. Back in the day, people were duped by an organization claiming to raise money for a one-way trip to Mars. Millions signed up then. **Finding a dozen people who don’t care to live beyond the mission isn’t difficult. *

*Why do these kids do this? I doubt they’re wanting to leave an empty Earth; they’re somehow drawn to the empty void of space more than the abundance of Earth. A true mystery there. They must have families, significant others, friends and interests outside that of space exploration. Yet, they’re choosing to throw it all away for this. Is uncovering the greatest mystery yet discovered in humanity’s short history **really worth** throwing your entire life away? *

*So, they’re the optimists and I’m the pessimist but we’re all somehow in the same boat. Or rather, the same ship. The same damn spaceship. Completely different spectrums of seeing what Earth has to offer but all wanting to leave it. Isn’t that a damn irony?*

He stopped writing as his eye caught a plastic facsimile of a glass bottle of Topo Chico strapped to the corner of his desk. It wasn’t a real glass bottle; they would never allow glass in beverage containers on a spacecraft. That was his favorite sparkling water. When he was feeling melancholy, it also tends to carry his mind to the blackened edges of a memory from a decade ago.

He was freshly divorced then and was living in a one-bedroom luxury apartment by himself. There was a balcony on the ground floor with a fountain laid in front of him. It was a private courtyard shared by the residents of the complex which lived on the inner side of the apartment building. He enjoyed sitting out there just watching the fountain and letting his mind wander while he drank his Topo Chico slowly. After the chaos of the marriage and the divorce process, he relished this calm. That day with its drizzling gray sky with light rain resonated with his mood – peaceful sadness.

A man with a dog walked into the courtyard. They wandered around a bit while the dog peed quietly in a corner on some fake grass. Presumably, the man was a neighbor who was just letting his dog out. This man probably had no intension of disturbing his peace. Yet, that mundane activity by another human being and another creature disturbed his peace immensely. He felt that he did not want to see another human being. 

He had quickly finished his Topo Chico and went inside to lay in bed staring at the ceiling daydreaming of having true peace. 

In the present, he put his pen on paper again.

*Everyone here had to go through a month of solitary confinement in a small self-sustaining 10 x 10 x **10 meter** cube to be considered even viable as a candidate. **Out of the battery of tests administered to candidates, this was the one where one was most likely to **weed out the **ones who didn’t want to go**. **About **80% didn’t pass it. Some went crazy **and begged to be released earlier **and some **removed their own applications afterwards though they technically passed.*

*Human** society** **as a whole has** declined **overall **to know and appreciate what it is like to be **truly **alone. **Technology has **permeated itself everywhere in the form of instant communication and information. **In the days before **the phone, **social **media,** augmented reality **and virtual worlds**, **one could sit quietly next to a fire and do nothing in the stillness of one’s mind.** Nowadays, that is a **far removed** reality where satellite signals **are able to** reach even the most remote jungle or desert.*

He paused as he recalled a time in his youth as part of a group tour taking an expedition to the Sahara Desert. One of the last vestiges of what truly remains remote in this world. It was considered one of the most remote parts remaining in the world. While he was fascinated by the sand dunes and the clear night sky, his peers were too busy recording their adventures and posting them online to even truly be present.

He thought it ironic that now that precisely because of all the noise that people have generated, we have caught the attention of a extraterrestrial intelligence. Here was a signal from an unknown source tapping humanity on the shoulder indubitably after watching countless hours of humanity’s self-generated social content from the information bubble that has been propagating outwards from the solar system for the last two centuries.

He wondered if this was happening to him right now. He had addressed the crew earlier and then informed them that he was going to rest in his cabin. With no urgent tasks at hand and being the captain, no one gainsaid his right for some privacy. Maybe he was a misanthrope to some degree. He frequently felt tired from seeing and interacting with people.

A loud beep erupted from the intercom on his desk which completely interrupted his thoughts. This was an indication that he was needed in the control room. He sighed. The time of the end for self-musing always seemed to come without warning. Responsibility and appearances are necessary. In slow methodical steps, he grasped the captain’s uniform strapped to his bed and carefully undid the straps. With one practiced motion, he donned it before it floated away.

There was a noticeable sense of unease he saw in the faces of several crewmembers that he passed by as he swam towards the control room. When he crossed over the threshold into the relatively large spherical room, everyone looked at him and gave him a salute. This was not a military vessel, but some traditions tend to follow along.

The six console controllers were already seated in front of their respective consoles. His second-in-command, a dark-haired Indian-American man with a name he has yet to fully able pronounce, stated, “Captain on the deck!”

He gave a nod and then drifted over to his seat in the center of the room. With practiced ease, he pulled himself on to the chair and strapped himself down in the seatbelts. Then he looked around and calmly asked, “What’s the situation?”

His second-in-command stated. “We are within range of communicating with the anomaly.”

“Vishwathmane, let’s call it Nexus 1 like protocol indicates. I understand that we’re all on first name basis here and like to keep things formal but since official exchanges are logged, we should stick with that formality at the very least.”

“Yes, captain. Nexus 1 is within our broadcasting range. Should we attempt contact?”

He nodded. “Initiate contact.”

The silence in the air lingered for far longer than anticipated. The screen on the communication screen showed no more blips indicating no signal. After a half minute of silence, a young woman with light brown hair sitting at the operations console with a sign that said COMMS spoke into her headset: “We have LOS.”

“Comms, how is this possible? Loss of signal isn’t expected for another three hours.” The questioning voice came from an older gentleman with a scruffy beard and bleary eyes. 

The comms operator calmly replied. “Captain, that would be the case if the comms relay satellite was working properly. Since it malfunctioned last week probably due to the incidence of that solar flare, we’ve had to send the signal direct from our antenna here.” 

“We’re using a high gain narrow beam antenna, no?” questioned the captain.

“Yes, we are. I suspect we have a navigation error in identifying where the spacecraft is, and our pointing might be inaccurate.”

“How much can Nexus 1 possibly deviate? It’s in a heliocentric elliptical orbit around the sun easily predictable in the short term by just an inverse square law approximation for the force. We had signal about an hour ago. There’s no conceivable way that the trajectory deviated that much from nominal in that short of an epoch span. Besides, we’re using the latest software that has all the gravitational forces of the outer planets and the moons considered. I don’t think you should blame Navigation. If anything, you should check your Comms system again. Maybe you misconfigured the radio.”

These snarky replies came from a middle-aged pudgy man with greasy hair sitting behind a console that read Navigation. He looked smugly at the Comms personnel in disbelief. She shook her head. “No, I checked the system several times. Everything is configured nominally on our end. Either the comms system on the spacecraft is not operational in which case there’ll be nothing we can do at all, or something perturbed its trajectory.”

“Maybe there was a momentum dump?” the captain asked. He continued answering his own question. “No, that’s not scheduled for another half-year. Besides, that little perturbation shouldn’t change the trajectory this much in just an hour.”

There was a sudden jump in the display. Startled, the woman working Comms quickly called out. “Captain, I am detecting a signal!”

“Comms, increase the gain,” the captain nodded.

“Just imagine, this is the first time an extraterrestrial civilization is hailing our spacecraft. I wonder what kind of message they will send!” these excited words hailed from a middle-aged woman behind a console that shone in bold letters SCIENCE.

“Chief Scientist Roberts, try to keep it professional here. Comms, what do you see?”

“One moment, Captain. The signal is not yet stable. The antenna is not performing as well as the nominal configuration.”

“I thought that was replaced last week with the emergency EVA last week after the SPE?”

“Well, the antenna is fine but it was always meant to be a backup and hence the receiver gain is only about half the power as the original.”

“I thought Avionics said we can use the other radio to strengthen the signal?”

“Avionics has assigned the task to someone, but the lab is understaffed and the someone is working on other high priority items first.”

“What could possibly be more important than our capability to communicate with the unknown spacecraft?”

“With all due respect, Captain, we are still trying to get back the DTE link. We have basic telemetry but no capacity to send any high-rate data back to Earth.”

The captain grimaced with annoyance. “If we need to allocate more human resources to get all our comm systems back up and running, we can do that after this. With only four technicians, maybe you can go back there yourself and assist.”

“With all due respect, I am not sure how much help I can be. The technicians know what they are doing. I will just be getting in their way.”

“Sitting on the bridge doesn’t mean that’s the only role you will be asked to play. Dr. Sandivar, as someone who graduated from MIT and worked in the space industry for over two decades, you’re telling me you know less than these technicians who went to community college.”

Everyone within earshot tensed up at the overtly condescending attitude displayed by the captain. He had also somehow put a sarcastic tinge on the Comms officer’s name. *Just because you went to school and worked doesn’t mean I should know everything.* she thought. 

Before she could respond in a more graceful manner than her thoughts, she was saved by a comment from a young man sitting for Ground Network. “Captain, we’re receiving an audiovisual signal.”

The captain turned to him. “Any chance of a virus infecting our system?”

He shook his head. “Negative, this is receive-only with no write capability.”

“In that case, Comms, put it on the big screen.”

There was a moment as Comms typed a few things on the console and clicked on a button. Three large monitors in the middle of the room now all displayed the same scene. There was the sound of static and speckled white noise. The captain grabbed a remote and turned up the volume. Coincidentally while he was doing so, the screen cleared and a scene was laid out in front of them in high-definition video. An androgynous voice started speaking describing the scene. 

Dr. Sandivar spoke aloud what was in everyone’s minds, “This shouldn’t be possible with our data rate and latency…” 

“Quiet, this is something to be investigated later. Please record as much as you can. Let’s see what they’re wanting to say,” the captain said.

Dr. Sandivar nodded and punched a few keys on his console to start the recording. Everyone in the room stared raptly at what was unfolding in front of them. The voice indistinguishably male and female started with the words “Reproducing the past requires a sizzle in the air…”

# Chapter 2: The Break

*Reproducing the past requires a sizzle in the air.** **The air was crisp and a gentle breeze carried the faint scent of sand and sagebrush as two people laid side by side on a blanket. There were two nondescript knapsacks of a dull brown color next to each of them. To an unenlightened 21**st** century human, one looked like a prepubescent girl around the age of 8 and the other looked like a young boy freshly out of his teens. The same unenlightened individual would also assume that they were siblings given their remarkable facial resemblance and the fact that they looked to be East Asian. Perhaps these two were out camping in the desert enjoying the night sky away from the light pollution of metropolitan areas? Perhaps the knapsacks held their sleeping bags and the tent that they have yet to pitch up? The unenlightened 21**st** century **human** would be wrong on every front but for the sake of placing labels on characters, let us address them as brother and sister for now.*

*The blanket looked much too thin to provide much comfort on the rocky ground filled with pebbles and small vegetation. Inscribed on the top right corner of the blanket in tiny script were imprinted the alphanumeric sequence CJ2127. Neither of them showed any discomfort. The two gazed at the starlit sky in silence until the presumed girl broke it with a question.*

*“Can you read me some poetry?”*

*The **boy smiled and picked up **a book that was **previously hidden **under **the **blanket. **“Sure, **the poetry section of this story will **start on page 99.**”*

*“Does that one **include** the **one about the eclipse**?”*

*“No, that would be in the dream of the eclipse described in chapter 3**. **There are three different versions** documented** and one of them has the **poem**.”*

*“That’s okay. You can read me that one later.”*

*The boy started reading. “Let’s start with the **one called Milky Way. **The Milky Way is **fun-sized** as the candy bar says**…”*

*The boy continued reading for **another **ten minutes before the girl yawned and **said**. “That’s enough for now. More can come later. **You don’t want to bore the audience.”*

*In an eerily creepy fashion, both stood up and turned towards the camera** as if addressing the crew. “I hope none of you are bored by our literary forays. I promise you this will be a good story with every **video **you receive.”*

The screen then cut off into blankness and the narration voice stopped as well. For a moment, everyone was stunned in confused silence and then immediately all started talking. The captain raised a hand and they all quieted. “One at a time if you have anything to say.”

“What the hell was that?” the navigator Kara immediately blurted.

“I don’t know. Do we have all the video recorded?” the captain asked.

“Yes,” Dr. Sandivar replied. “It was difficult but we managed to download it all while it was transmitting but how such a clear signal could be sent with such a low data rate is unclear to me.”

“This is a real headscratcher but I trust that the expertise of those onboard can figure this out given time. For now, let’s make sure we can safely get this data into the appropriate hands. Does anyone have an immediate idea of what we just saw?”

Vishwathmane shook his head. “Captain, I’m still trying to compose my thoughts. The poetry wasn’t terrible but it wasn’t the quality level that my English professor would have considered world-class.”

The navigator snorted. “And how would you know how to rate poetry?”

Vishwathmane stiffened and gave a man a glare. “Officer Jones, I would have you know that I double majored in both electrical engineering as well as classic literature.”

“A rare specimen indeed for such a motley crew,” the chief scientist remarked and a few people chuckled.

The captain cleared his throat. “Dr. Roberts, please quiet down. All of you are acting like a group of school children instead of supposedly what the best humanity have to offer.”

Turning back to the Chief Officer, he said, “Is that all?”

“Well, I was trying to assess the origins of the accents provided by the two children as well as their ethnic appearance, but that part is a complete mystery. The pronunciation indicates no particular country of origin. Perhaps our ethnographer would know but since we have no recording there’s not much we can do to get her perspective.”

The captain nodded. “Vishwathmane, after this meeting, find our ethnographer and give her this data to look into. For the rest of us, we still need to navigate this ship and send this transmission to Earth for our experts there to dissect as well. Comms, please prepare to compress and send this entire video to the next available ground station.”

“Uh, the next one wouldn’t be for another three hours. Orbital geometry is a bitch and the Earth won’t rotate any faster. Of course, it’ll probably take me a few hours to compress and save the data in the right format.”

“Then get started on that. We don’t have time to waste.”

He snapped his finger and everyone got to work.

# Chapter 3: The Dream

Navigator Kara had a dream that night about a solar eclipse. It was like something she saw when she was a little girl of about 8 but surprisingly different than the experience she recounted. It felt like several different memories of others pieced together and she was experiencing them all at once. When she awoke, before the dream scattered into nothingness, she grabbed her voice recorder beside her bed and started narrating.

STARTING RECORDING:

I was lying outside under the shade of a tree on a sunny day reading from the book *Einstein and the Total Eclipse** *by Peter Coles in the chapter on the Anatomy of an Eclipse. I heard myself read these words:

“But the possibility of having a total eclipse relies on one extraordinary and fascinating coincidence. The Moon is much closer to us than the Sun is (250,000 miles compared to 93 million), but is also very much smaller (its diameter is just over 2000 miles, while the sun is about 900,000 miles across). Somehow these numbers have conspired with the laws of trigonometry to produce a situation in which the apparent size of the Moon’s disk is almost exactly the same as that of the Sun. There is no convincing explanation of how this remarkable coincidence came about, but without it total eclipses would be impossible.”

I paused and took out a pen. I found a blank page on a notepad and drew a diagram on there to do some basic calculations to confirm what the author stated. I know the moon is approximately 1080 miles in radius and the sun is approximately 433,000 miles in radius so I will use those values instead of the book-given one. 

Calculations indicate angular radius of moon to be 0.25 degrees and that of sun to be 0.27 degrees. However, since the distance to the moon and sun both varies since the orbit is elliptical and not perfectly circular, there are epoch intervals when both of those coincide with each other. 

This was simple enough math but sometimes it feels good to prove things by paper and pencil instead of just trusting what is written on paper.

The air suddenly grew cooler. Is there a storm coming? I looked up and saw that the sun was slowly being occluded by a shadowy object would could only be the moon. “Strange! I didn’t think there was a solar eclipse predicted for today. It’s not even the new moon and eclipses can only happen during the new moon.”

An unbidden voice came to her mind and instinctively she knew these were the words written by Annie Dillard on the impossibility of capturing a sight via a photograph. *The lenses of telescopes and cameras can no **more** cover the breadth and scale of the visual array than language can cover the breadth and simultaneity of internal experience. Lenses enlarge the sight, omit its context, and make of it a pretty and sensible picture, like something on a Christmas card.*

Who was Annie Dillard? I think she’s an American author who wrote a lot about nature poetically with frequent references to science. I’m not known to remember quotes by anyone so being able to recite this from memory felt strange.

What was equally peculiar is to see everything in the crystal light of sudden twilight converging towards darkness. There’s also a feeling of impending doom. The eclipse should only last a few minutes and then it would be over but that wasn’t the feeling I felt then. I felt it would come and then last forever. This would have contradicted every aspect of celestial mechanics but that feeling resided there. The moon cannot stop its motion or else it would quickly collide with Earth due to gravity. It was in perpetual free fall. The only way would be for the moon to stay exactly where it is while blocking the sun would be for it to also counteract gravity exactly by accelerating in a direction radially outwards.

These thoughts, useless as they might be, were going through my brain while observing this phenomenon. I recalled years ago at a tender age of 8 seeing the 2024 solar eclipse in Dallas, TX. That was the pivotal moment then which led me to want to learn more about celestial motion. All that led me down the path of studying physics and then later aerospace.

But what does all this have to do with now? Simply that the eclipse was not disappearing. It seemed to persist. A sudden bell-like gong rang in my mind. It reverberated. Then, a calm feminine voice akin to one from a meditation video or a yoga teacher resonated in my mind. 

*There is no life without change. **Read A Day Curated** in the Poetry section of the book** you are part of**.*

The words made no sense to me. The sky was still half-black in that eclipse twilight. “What does that mean?”

The voice only repeated itself again with the same words.

“This is not helping. What book is this and what do you mean I’m part of a book?”

Once again, there was no change in the words that continued to be spoken in my mind. Just as I was about to feel like I was slowly going insane, the voice stopped. At the same time, time seemed to have started again. I could see a tiny sliver of the sun poking through the previously completely covered up circle. The moon was moving again.

A whisper began very close to my ears. “Dynamic balance is essential to the way of the unity of opposites.”

It was a different voice than before. This was not spoken calmly but in a sense of desperate urgency. Startled, I looked around but saw no one in my vicinity. More whispers started from an ever-growing number of invisible entities. Some seemed to be right next to my ears while others seemed to be inside my skull. 

The voices repeated abstract notions. “The unity is a process which now lets in the dark and now the light. Like the orbit of a planet around a star, there is periapsis and there is apoapsis. There is distance and there is nearness. Everything changes in the system but the system itself does not change.” 

“Who are you? What do you want?” I yelled.

There was no acknowledgement. The voices only continued. “Consider even the motion of a simple ball moving clockwise around a circle with constant speed. Project its motion into a 1D plane as if capturing a shadow. In the projection, it slows down as it reaches the edge, turns around and accelerates again only to slow down once more – and so the endless cycle continues. An oscillation between two opposite points. Is this evident to you?”

Hoping the voice would stop, I said. “Give me a moment and let me do the math. For simplicity, I’ll consider the initial condition at t=0 to be at (r,0).”

I flipped to the next page of my notepad and wrote down the Cartesian coordinates describing uniform circular motion. Then, I looked at the y-component of it and took the derivative.

y=-rωcosωt

ω=2πTwhere T is the period

where the Greek letter omega is the angular velocity defined as radians per second, t is the time in seconds and r is the radius of the circle. 

I narrated as I described the results, “In this equation, y-dot (the y-component of the velocity which is also the shadow velocity) is 0 when t is a quarter of the period or three quarters of the period. It is maximum when t is equal to 0 or half of the period. Translated into position space, this means that the shadow moves fastest when the ball is at (+r,0) or (-r,0) and it moves slowest when the ball is at (0, -r) and (0, +r).”

“You can do the math but do you realize the deeper meaning with this analogy?” 

I felt that I knew the answer but I was not 100% certain. I replied. “I can guess but why don’t you tell me?”

There as a brief pause before the voice continued. “Polarities that exists in lower dimensions do not exist in higher dimensions. The extremity of the velocity observed in 1D is but uniformity from a 2D perspective.”

I nodded. This is still insane but at least a conversation was happening. “You mean to say that we cannot understand why opposites consistently occur in our world but this is because we lack an understanding from our limited perspective. This is one of the oldest concepts in human philosophy and religion. The nature of duality and the concept of change. What does this have to do with me?”

 

# Chapter 4: The Cantina

Sometimes, life can take you to the oddest of places, thought Megan. If twelve summers ago, someone had told her that she would be on a spacecraft flying towards the first confirmed extraterrestrial vessel, she would have construed it as pure nonsense. This isn’t because she didn’t believe in the existence of aliens but rather because she didn’t believe she had any skills that would warrant her a position on a ship.

She took a sip from her flask of whiskey as she took out her journal to write. She was glad that she was able to find a quiet corner of what the crew affectionately called the cantina. Due to the efficiency of space requirements, it served as a meal hall as well with about half a dozen round tables. Not many people came here during this time of the sleep cycle which served her just fine. Thoughts flourished better in solitude.

*If this was on Earth or on a research station at Phobos, I would be **taking a walk first to marinate my thoughts. # Chapter 5: The Message

What was mentioned was the on-going fight between the two countries over resources and control of the mines. More villagers and towns as statistical numbers were quoted. Some images and videos were shown of the aftermath of the carnage. No illegal weaponry were used with UN Peacekeepers monitoring but conventional approved weaponry were enough these days to cause significant bloodshed.

Shen scoffed. The news these days was worthless. What was not mentioned between the lines was that all the mines on Earth and Luna were running low on resources. Mars alone did not have enough to continue sending the amount necessary to support the 12 trillion people in the Earth-Luna system not even including all the mostly self-sustaining orbital space stations. The technology to mine asteroids have been around for nearly a century now but only now with resource wars happening were visionaries finally getting the funds needed to explore how to do them economically.

Another sign of the short-sightedness of humanity. No one truly cared when the scientists and environmental activist groups warned that the ocean levels were rising every year with increasing temperature until major cities in the powerful nations of the world were starting to be submerged underwater.

Hence, this was why two months after the last update on February 14, 2026, he picked up his metaphorical pen and decided to continue writing the story despite his worry that much of it might end up being drivel with a discombobulated plot.
