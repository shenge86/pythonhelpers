# Chapter 2: The Break

*Reproducing the past requires a sizzle in the air.** **The air was crisp and a gentle breeze carried the faint scent of sand and sagebrush as two people laid side by side on a blanket. There were two nondescript knapsacks of a dull brown color next to each of them. To an unenlightened 21**st** century human, one looked like a prepubescent girl around the age of 8 and the other looked like a young boy freshly out of his teens. The same unenlightened individual would also assume that they were siblings given their remarkable facial resemblance and the fact that they looked to be East Asian. Perhaps these two were out camping in the desert enjoying the night sky away from the light pollution of metropolitan areas? Perhaps the knapsacks held their sleeping bags and the tent that they have yet to pitch up? The unenlightened 21**st** century **human** would be wrong on every front but for the sake of placing labels on characters, let us address them as brother and sister for now.*

*The blanket looked much too thin to provide much comfort on the rocky ground filled with pebbles and small vegetation. Inscribed on the top right corner of the blanket in tiny script were imprinted the alphanumeric sequence CJ2127. Neither of them showed any discomfort. The two gazed at the starlit sky in silence until the presumed girl broke it with a question.*

*“Can you read me some poetry?”*

*The **boy smiled and picked up **a book that was **previously hidden **under **the **blanket. **“Sure, **the poetry section of this story will **start on page 99.**”*

*“Does that one **include** the **one about the eclipse**?”*

*“No, that would be in the dream of the eclipse described in chapter 3**. **There are three different versions** documented** and one of them has the **poem**.”*

*“That’s okay. You can read me that one later.”*

*The boy started reading. “Let’s start with the **one called Milky Way. **The Milky Way is **fun-sized** as the candy bar says**…”*

*The boy continued reading for **another **ten minutes before the girl yawned and **said**. “That’s enough for now. More can come later. **You don’t want to bore the audience.”*

*In an eerily creepy fashion, both stood up and turned towards the camera** as if addressing the crew. “I hope none of you are bored by our literary forays. I promise you this will be a good story with every **video **you receive.”*

The screen then cut off into blankness and the narration voice stopped as well. For a moment, everyone was stunned in confused silence and then immediately all started talking. The captain raised a hand and they all quieted. “One at a time if you have anything to say.”

“What the hell was that?” the navigator Kara immediately blurted.

“I don’t know. Do we have all the video recorded?” the captain asked.

“Yes,” Dr. Sandivar replied. “It was difficult but we managed to download it all while it was transmitting but how such a clear signal could be sent with such a low data rate is unclear to me.”

“This is a real headscratcher but I trust that the expertise of those onboard can figure this out given time. For now, let’s make sure we can safely get this data into the appropriate hands. Does anyone have an immediate idea of what we just saw?”

Vishwathmane shook his head. “Captain, I’m still trying to compose my thoughts. The poetry wasn’t terrible but it wasn’t the quality level that my English professor would have considered world-class.”

The navigator snorted. “And how would you know how to rate poetry?”

Vishwathmane stiffened and gave a man a glare. “Officer Jones, I would have you know that I double majored in both electrical engineering as well as classic literature.”

“A rare specimen indeed for such a motley crew,” the chief scientist remarked and a few people chuckled.

The captain cleared his throat. “Dr. Roberts, please quiet down. All of you are acting like a group of school children instead of supposedly what the best humanity have to offer.”

Turning back to the Chief Officer, he said, “Is that all?”

“Well, I was trying to assess the origins of the accents provided by the two children as well as their ethnic appearance, but that part is a complete mystery. The pronunciation indicates no particular country of origin. Perhaps our ethnographer would know but since we have no recording there’s not much we can do to get her perspective.”

The captain nodded. “Vishwathmane, after this meeting, find our ethnographer and give her this data to look into. For the rest of us, we still need to navigate this ship and send this transmission to Earth for our experts there to dissect as well. Comms, please prepare to compress and send this entire video to the next available ground station.”

“Uh, the next one wouldn’t be for another three hours. Orbital geometry is a bitch and the Earth won’t rotate any faster. Of course, it’ll probably take me a few hours to compress and save the data in the right format.”

“Then get started on that. We don’t have time to waste.”

He snapped his finger and everyone got to work.
