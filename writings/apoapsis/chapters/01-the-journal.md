# Chapter 1: The Journal

A lone man sat at his metal desk basked under the dim glow of a desk lamp. The window outside was pitch black though he thought he could see at least a few notable stars. Perhaps that red dot at the far edge was Mars. The sun was on the opposite side of the spacecraft which he accepted as an unbidden celestial gift. Thoughts seemed to come easier in darkness. He picked up a yellow legal pad and started to write. Words formed. 

*Simple words are enough to describe this feeling. Is it emptiness? Excitement? Melancholy? Melancholy and emptiness are similar in nature. **A particular type of ennui. **Cousins almost or rather a parent-child relationship. One begets another. A subclass maybe if you’re into object-oriented programming.*

*The window outside showed only darkness. A void deeper than what most of humanity has seen. But is it deeper than the void I felt within? I felt that there was nothing more for me to do back on Earth. Parents deceased. Relatives alive are those I barely know more than the barista at the local Starbucks. Divorced. Not talking to ex-wife except for the kids. Kids **grown** up now. Never did **connect** well with them either. May be generational gap. Maybe he’s just a **maverick** too obsessed with his own inner thoughts and hobbies and interests and travels to really connect with anyone on their level.*

*What do I know? There’s a great deal of communication that passes as “normal” social interaction which bores me. I was always the odd one out even if I was the most popular in some ways – a gala dinner in my honor for instance. I said my inspiring words and then spent the next few hours in insipid vapid conversations. Everyone saw just images of me that they thought they knew. When I tried to speak my true thoughts to family, they just thought of me as an alien. *

*Might as we**ll be **one** right? In my entire life, I have yet to meet someone who I felt truly **understands me**. Maybe that’s why I signed up for this voyage. Volunteered. Applied. To see possible real aliens or at least the remnants of their technology.*

*Humans are draining social creatures. The solution I found was to spend **less and less** time with all of them. The government must be surprised that it didn’t need to try very hard to convince me to go on this one-way trip. I knew even before they told me. There’s not enough fuel to get there and back. *

*How many others on this ship **knows** this? We have a crew of a dozen people. At least some of them must be smart enough to know this. Maybe they just don’t care. Back in the day, people were duped by an organization claiming to raise money for a one-way trip to Mars. Millions signed up then. **Finding a dozen people who don’t care to live beyond the mission isn’t difficult. *

*Why do these kids do this? I doubt they’re wanting to leave an empty Earth; they’re somehow drawn to the empty void of space more than the abundance of Earth. A true mystery there. They must have families, significant others, friends and interests outside that of space exploration. Yet, they’re choosing to throw it all away for this. Is uncovering the greatest mystery yet discovered in humanity’s short history **really worth** throwing your entire life away? *

*So, they’re the optimists and I’m the pessimist but we’re all somehow in the same boat. Or rather, the same ship. The same damn spaceship. Completely different spectrums of seeing what Earth has to offer but all wanting to leave it. Isn’t that a damn irony?*

He stopped writing as his eye caught a plastic facsimile of a glass bottle of Topo Chico strapped to the corner of his desk. It wasn’t a real glass bottle; they would never allow glass in beverage containers on a spacecraft. That was his favorite sparkling water. When he was feeling melancholy, it also tends to carry his mind to the blackened edges of a memory from a decade ago.

He was freshly divorced then and was living in a one-bedroom luxury apartment by himself. There was a balcony on the ground floor with a fountain laid in front of him. It was a private courtyard shared by the residents of the complex which lived on the inner side of the apartment building. He enjoyed sitting out there just watching the fountain and letting his mind wander while he drank his Topo Chico slowly. After the chaos of the marriage and the divorce process, he relished this calm. That day with its drizzling gray sky with light rain resonated with his mood – peaceful sadness.

A man with a dog walked into the courtyard. They wandered around a bit while the dog peed quietly in a corner on some fake grass. Presumably, the man was a neighbor who was just letting his dog out. This man probably had no intension of disturbing his peace. Yet, that mundane activity by another human being and another creature disturbed his peace immensely. He felt that he did not want to see another human being. 

He had quickly finished his Topo Chico and went inside to lay in bed staring at the ceiling daydreaming of having true peace. 

In the present, he put his pen on paper again.

*Everyone here had to go through a month of solitary confinement in a small self-sustaining 10 x 10 x **10 meter** cube to be considered even viable as a candidate. **Out of the battery of tests administered to candidates, this was the one where one was most likely to **weed out the **ones who didn’t want to go**. **About **80% didn’t pass it. Some went crazy **and begged to be released earlier **and some **removed their own applications afterwards though they technically passed.*

*Human** society** **as a whole has** declined **overall **to know and appreciate what it is like to be **truly **alone. **Technology has **permeated itself everywhere in the form of instant communication and information. **In the days before **the phone, **social **media,** augmented reality **and virtual worlds**, **one could sit quietly next to a fire and do nothing in the stillness of one’s mind.** Nowadays, that is a **far removed** reality where satellite signals **are able to** reach even the most remote jungle or desert.*

He paused as he recalled a time in his youth as part of a group tour taking an expedition to the Sahara Desert. One of the last vestiges of what truly remains remote in this world. It was considered one of the most remote parts remaining in the world. While he was fascinated by the sand dunes and the clear night sky, his peers were too busy recording their adventures and posting them online to even truly be present.

He thought it ironic that now that precisely because of all the noise that people have generated, we have caught the attention of a extraterrestrial intelligence. Here was a signal from an unknown source tapping humanity on the shoulder indubitably after watching countless hours of humanity’s self-generated social content from the information bubble that has been propagating outwards from the solar system for the last two centuries.

He wondered if this was happening to him right now. He had addressed the crew earlier and then informed them that he was going to rest in his cabin. With no urgent tasks at hand and being the captain, no one gainsaid his right for some privacy. Maybe he was a misanthrope to some degree. He frequently felt tired from seeing and interacting with people.

A loud beep erupted from the intercom on his desk which completely interrupted his thoughts. This was an indication that he was needed in the control room. He sighed. The time of the end for self-musing always seemed to come without warning. Responsibility and appearances are necessary. In slow methodical steps, he grasped the captain’s uniform strapped to his bed and carefully undid the straps. With one practiced motion, he donned it before it floated away.

There was a noticeable sense of unease he saw in the faces of several crewmembers that he passed by as he swam towards the control room. When he crossed over the threshold into the relatively large spherical room, everyone looked at him and gave him a salute. This was not a military vessel, but some traditions tend to follow along.

The six console controllers were already seated in front of their respective consoles. His second-in-command, a dark-haired Indian-American man with a name he has yet to fully able pronounce, stated, “Captain on the deck!”

He gave a nod and then drifted over to his seat in the center of the room. With practiced ease, he pulled himself on to the chair and strapped himself down in the seatbelts. Then he looked around and calmly asked, “What’s the situation?”

His second-in-command stated. “We are within range of communicating with the anomaly.”

“Vishwathmane, let’s call it Nexus 1 like protocol indicates. I understand that we’re all on first name basis here and like to keep things formal but since official exchanges are logged, we should stick with that formality at the very least.”

“Yes, captain. Nexus 1 is within our broadcasting range. Should we attempt contact?”

He nodded. “Initiate contact.”

The silence in the air lingered for far longer than anticipated. The screen on the communication screen showed no more blips indicating no signal. After a half minute of silence, a young woman with light brown hair sitting at the operations console with a sign that said COMMS spoke into her headset: “We have LOS.”

“Comms, how is this possible? Loss of signal isn’t expected for another three hours.” The questioning voice came from an older gentleman with a scruffy beard and bleary eyes. 

The comms operator calmly replied. “Captain, that would be the case if the comms relay satellite was working properly. Since it malfunctioned last week probably due to the incidence of that solar flare, we’ve had to send the signal direct from our antenna here.” 

“We’re using a high gain narrow beam antenna, no?” questioned the captain.

“Yes, we are. I suspect we have a navigation error in identifying where the spacecraft is, and our pointing might be inaccurate.”

“How much can Nexus 1 possibly deviate? It’s in a heliocentric elliptical orbit around the sun easily predictable in the short term by just an inverse square law approximation for the force. We had signal about an hour ago. There’s no conceivable way that the trajectory deviated that much from nominal in that short of an epoch span. Besides, we’re using the latest software that has all the gravitational forces of the outer planets and the moons considered. I don’t think you should blame Navigation. If anything, you should check your Comms system again. Maybe you misconfigured the radio.”

These snarky replies came from a middle-aged pudgy man with greasy hair sitting behind a console that read Navigation. He looked smugly at the Comms personnel in disbelief. She shook her head. “No, I checked the system several times. Everything is configured nominally on our end. Either the comms system on the spacecraft is not operational in which case there’ll be nothing we can do at all, or something perturbed its trajectory.”

“Maybe there was a momentum dump?” the captain asked. He continued answering his own question. “No, that’s not scheduled for another half-year. Besides, that little perturbation shouldn’t change the trajectory this much in just an hour.”

There was a sudden jump in the display. Startled, the woman working Comms quickly called out. “Captain, I am detecting a signal!”

“Comms, increase the gain,” the captain nodded.

“Just imagine, this is the first time an extraterrestrial civilization is hailing our spacecraft. I wonder what kind of message they will send!” these excited words hailed from a middle-aged woman behind a console that shone in bold letters SCIENCE.

“Chief Scientist Roberts, try to keep it professional here. Comms, what do you see?”

“One moment, Captain. The signal is not yet stable. The antenna is not performing as well as the nominal configuration.”

“I thought that was replaced last week with the emergency EVA last week after the SPE?”

“Well, the antenna is fine but it was always meant to be a backup and hence the receiver gain is only about half the power as the original.”

“I thought Avionics said we can use the other radio to strengthen the signal?”

“Avionics has assigned the task to someone, but the lab is understaffed and the someone is working on other high priority items first.”

“What could possibly be more important than our capability to communicate with the unknown spacecraft?”

“With all due respect, Captain, we are still trying to get back the DTE link. We have basic telemetry but no capacity to send any high-rate data back to Earth.”

The captain grimaced with annoyance. “If we need to allocate more human resources to get all our comm systems back up and running, we can do that after this. With only four technicians, maybe you can go back there yourself and assist.”

“With all due respect, I am not sure how much help I can be. The technicians know what they are doing. I will just be getting in their way.”

“Sitting on the bridge doesn’t mean that’s the only role you will be asked to play. Dr. Sandivar, as someone who graduated from MIT and worked in the space industry for over two decades, you’re telling me you know less than these technicians who went to community college.”

Everyone within earshot tensed up at the overtly condescending attitude displayed by the captain. He had also somehow put a sarcastic tinge on the Comms officer’s name. *Just because you went to school and worked doesn’t mean I should know everything.* she thought. 

Before she could respond in a more graceful manner than her thoughts, she was saved by a comment from a young man sitting for Ground Network. “Captain, we’re receiving an audiovisual signal.”

The captain turned to him. “Any chance of a virus infecting our system?”

He shook his head. “Negative, this is receive-only with no write capability.”

“In that case, Comms, put it on the big screen.”

There was a moment as Comms typed a few things on the console and clicked on a button. Three large monitors in the middle of the room now all displayed the same scene. There was the sound of static and speckled white noise. The captain grabbed a remote and turned up the volume. Coincidentally while he was doing so, the screen cleared and a scene was laid out in front of them in high-definition video. An androgynous voice started speaking describing the scene. 

Dr. Sandivar spoke aloud what was in everyone’s minds, “This shouldn’t be possible with our data rate and latency…” 

“Quiet, this is something to be investigated later. Please record as much as you can. Let’s see what they’re wanting to say,” the captain said.

Dr. Sandivar nodded and punched a few keys on his console to start the recording. Everyone in the room stared raptly at what was unfolding in front of them. The voice indistinguishably male and female started with the words “Reproducing the past requires a sizzle in the air…”
