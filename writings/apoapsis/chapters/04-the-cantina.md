# Chapter 4: The Cantina

Sometimes, life can take you to the oddest of places, thought Megan. If twelve summers ago, someone had told her that she would be on a spacecraft flying towards the first confirmed extraterrestrial vessel, she would have construed it as pure nonsense. This isn’t because she didn’t believe in the existence of aliens but rather because she didn’t believe she had any skills that would warrant her a position on a ship.

She took a sip from her flask of whiskey as she took out her journal to write. She was glad that she was able to find a quiet corner of what the crew affectionately called the cantina. Due to the efficiency of space requirements, it served as a meal hall as well with about half a dozen round tables. Not many people came here during this time of the sleep cycle which served her just fine. Thoughts flourished better in solitude.

*If this was on Earth or on a research station at Phobos, I would be **taking a walk first to marinate my thoughts. Seeing that I don’t have the luxury of space on this ship, I made do with some **walks **on the personal treadmill**. **Sometimes, I feel** that I really shouldn’t be here or maybe rather it’s apt to say that my intention to come here isn’t as pure as people may think. **I love space but I don’t think I would have gathered enough courage to leave if **it wasn’t because of how stifling I was starting to feel.*

*My life **was **becoming **stifling** and I **didn’t want to keep drowning in the chaos of human relationships. **Love is a word and concept which I thought I knew what it meant **from all my reflections and **therapy** **but it has proven time and time again to be difficult to truly offer and receive in a health way. *

*Am I a coward for **cho**osing** to say bon voyage and **leave this world? **Those who have more patience or faith would try **a bit more. **Do I give up too easily or am I just unlucky in landing in the same situation time after time? **To leave is sometimes the easiest option. **Some people would move to another state, country**, the moon or Mars.** I chose to take it to the next level.** I threw a dart in the dark never expecting to **actually be** picked up by this project.*

“And yet, here I am, whereas possibly worthier candidates are residing on Earth. And all because of one book I wrote,” she mused aloud to herself as she took another sip from her drink.

“And what a book it was!” a voice continued for her.

Megan jolted with a startle and looked up from her journal. She saw a tall brunette with a broad grin standing in front of her. “Navigator Kara, what are you doing here?”

“No formalities needed. I’m just Kara to you. Another ship mate ready to wind down just like anyone else, I suppose. After that garbled mess, I needed to get a drink. Mind if I sit down?” she gestured to the empty chair in front of her.

Megan shook her head. “Go ahead. I’m not expecting company.”

“Well, you’re definitely the most pleasant of the bunch. An outsider of a sort I would say.”

“You’re quite blunt, aren’t you?”

“One of my many charming qualities as the captain would point out,” Kara said with a sigh. “I don’t suppose you worked out the meaning of it all. You did write a book on it.”

“The messages from the aliens?” the word alien still sounded strange and foreign to her tongue but she forced it out for lack of a more proper phrase.

Kara nodded. “No one can make sense of it. As the resident ET expert, I thought you might have some idea who went with the hard sciences.”

Megan shook her head. “I’m still working on it. It’s not that simple. There seems to be messages within messages. An enigma wrapped inside another one and possibly another one. Who knows how many layers there are?”

“Not a Klein bottle I hope.”

“A Klein what?”

“Bad math joke. It’s a 3D object hat has only one surface. Read some topology back in the day.”

Megan nodded. She thought of an ex of hers who loved math but she wasn’t sure she wanted to bring that up. I do wonder why he wasn’t selected to go on this mission, she thought.

“There’s another reason I wanted to talk to you but I think you might think I’m crazy.”

“I’m crazy enough to have written a book on how to communicate with aliens who don’t use verbal communication. Try me.”

Kara laughed. “You make a fair point there. I can toast to that.”

She raised her mug of fizzy beer and Megan raised her glass of whiskey. After they chugged down a gulp, Kara grimaced. “Definitely you have better taste than me. Where did you get that any way?”

“I brought it with me. Didn’t know what that would have on the ship. Kentucky’s finest. I grew up drinking it.”

“Well, you certainly have foresight. I just assumed that we’ll be too busy to do any serious drinking. If you’re willing to share, after I finish this crap, maybe you can pour me a bit.”

“Sure, I still have a few more bottles so I don’t mind sharing.”

“Just take it easy and no need to be too cavalier in showing it. You don’t want the whole crew to know you have some good stuff. But let me get back on topic. It’s about the dream that I had last night.”

“Tell me more,” Megan said with eyes widening in curiosity.

“The dream started with a solar eclipse but it was strange since there seemed to be a narrator narrating the sequence of events while it was happening.”

“A narrator?”

“Yes, a female voice that rang in my ears and described what I was seeing. I felt like I was watching a movie and reading a book at the same time. What’s worse is that the perspective kept changing. One moment I was someone on the ground looking up. The next moment I was in the sky in an airplane. I think at another moment I was in space on the moon. It felt disorienting and alien.”

“How long was the dream?”

“It felt like forever but maybe half an hour?”

“Describe it to me in full detail.”

With that prompt, Kara repeated the scenery that played in her dream. When she was done, she waited for the ethnographer to say something.

“I believe this is a form of communication? It may be related to what we’ve experienced so far on this ship.”

“How? It doesn’t make any sense. Through dreams and vague mathematical metaphors?”

“We humans have common languages and cultural repertoires which allow us to have a common platform of using any of the myriad human tongues to communicate. But imagine if we’re trying to communicate with an ant. Ants know how to communicate with each other not through any language that can be written down or spoken but rather through chemicals. 

Now, if an intelligent ant is smart enough to be able to learn science and build technology, they must also realize that other lifeforms don’t necessarily communicate the same way they do. However, since the gulf between how they communicate, which may be intuitive, is so vastly different from direct singular sentences which humans communicate, what else can they do but translate it into mediums depicting visual and auditory scenes which best approximate how they communicate in their own way? Mathematics is an universal language but it’s difficult to convey human emotions without some context around it. Maybe that’s why these scenes are so abstract. It’s their way to try to convey meaning by providing context around universal truths.”

“So you’re saying these aliens are like ants?”

“Not at all. I’m using ants as an example since they represent a species which obviously can communicate with each other in very complex ways and maintain cohesion while being completely incomprehensible to us. Any alien species would most likely have a language and communication structure completely alien, pun intended, to us.”

“But what about the poem and the book? What does it mean I’m part of a book? I looked up the name of that poem and couldn’t find it anywhere.”

“Maybe it’s a metaphor that we’re all part of a bigger story. I’m not sure why a particular reference to an unknown poem is mentioned.”

“If we can find the book, maybe things would make more sense.”

“Clarity can be hard when trying to communicate with foreign cultures. If you remind me again the name of the poem, I can see what I can do.”

“The poem is called A Day Curated. I don’t know what the book name is.”

Megan pursed her lips. “In my view, the book is probably an allegory to the life you or all of us are leading right now. There may not be a title. Interesting word choice though for the title of the book. There may be something more there. Have you had any more dreams or encounters of such words?”

“No, just this one dream. I’ve been keeping an eye out on any scraps of words or anything describing eclipses but so far nothing has come to my attention.”

“I’ll keep an eye out too and I’ll talk to some others of the crew. Maybe I can put together the pieces of this puzzle. Let me know if you have more dreams.”

“I guess that’s why they hired you to be on the ship.”

“They almost didn’t. It was a last minute hire.” Megan said simply. She didn’t want to go into the details of how that transpired.

A momentary pause as Kara realized that this might be a sensitive question. She cleared her throat to clear the air. “You think this is something I should report to the Captain?”

“You’re higher ranked than me. I would think so if we’re trying to understand their motivations enough and communicate with them. Maybe you can also inquire if anyone else on this ship is also having similar or other dreams.”

“That’s not a bad idea. Thank you, Megan.”

Kara took one last swig of the cup and got up. “I need to get some rest before my next shift but I’ll bring it up next time I see the Captain on deck.”

Megan smiled. “Take care of yourself, Kara. I can’t say I’m completely right but it’s just a hunch and I’ve been told my hunches have been more right than wrong.”

Kara returned her smile and nodded before she walked away. Megan sat there for a few minutes longer gathering her thoughts. She took out her paper-bound notebook and wrote down what just transpired as well as a few of her own theories. This all tied into the larger mystery of the media they’ve received so far from the alien spacecraft. But what did it all mean? Everyone on board was trying to decipher that but everyone else had other jobs to do to maintain or fly the ship. Her only job was to figure out these meanings. Not all the puzzle pieces are on the board but she’s gathering them.
